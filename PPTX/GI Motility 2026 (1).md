## Slide 2
Esophageal Motility Disorders

Types:

Achalasia
Hypercontractile (jackhammer) esophagus
Ineffective esophageal motility (IEM)

---

## Slide 3
Pathogenesis:

Achalasia: Loss of inhibitory nitrergic neurons in the myenteric plexus → failure of lower esophageal sphincter (LES) relaxation + absent peristalsis.
Hypercontractile: Imbalanced neural inhibition/excitation → simultaneous or excessively forceful contractions.
IEM: Weak or failed peristalsis, often post-inflammatory or idiopathic.

## Slide 5
Clinical Presentation:

Dysphagia (both solids and liquids in achalasia; intermittent in DES)
Regurgitation, chest pain (DES, jackhammer)
Heartburn (IEM)

---

## Slide 6
Treatment:

Achalasia: Pneumatic dilation, peroral endoscopic myotomy (POEM), laparoscopic Heller myotomy, botulinum toxin (temporary).
/Hypercontractile: Calcium channel blockers, nitrates, botox, POEM for refractory cases.
IEM: Treat underlying GERD; prokinetics (limited efficacy).

## Slide 7
2. Gastric Motility Disorders

Type:
Gastroparesis

Pathogenesis:
Impaired gastric emptying without mechanical obstruction.
Causes: diabetic autonomic neuropathy, post-viral, post-surgical (vagal injury), idiopathic, Parkinson’s.
Cellular defects: loss of interstitial cells of Cajal (pacemakers), neuronal nitric oxide deficiency.

---

## Slide 8
Clinical Presentation:

Early satiety, postprandial fullness, nausea, vomiting, bloating, abdominal pain.
May lead to bezoars, malnutrition, glycemic instability (in diabetes).

---

## Slide 9
Treatment:

Dietary: Small, low-fat, low-fiber meals.
Pharmacologic: Metoclopramide (prokinetic + antiemetic), domperidone, erythromycin (short-term).
Refractory: Gastric peroral endoscopic myotomy (G-POEM), gastric electrical stimulation, pyloroplasty.
Supportive: Antiemetics (ondansetron, promethazine).

---

## Slide 10
3. Small Bowel Motility Disorders

Types:
Chronic intestinal pseudo-obstruction (CIPO)
Superior mesenteric artery (SMA) syndrome

---

## Slide 11
Pathogenesis:

CIPO: Neuromuscular or myopathic failure (scleroderma, amyloidosis, paraneoplastic, mitochondrial disorders) → ineffective propulsion despite no mechanical blockage.
SMA syndrome: Loss of mesenteric fat pad (e.g., after rapid weight loss) → duodenal compression between aorta and SMA.

## Slide 13
Clinical Presentation:

Chronic bloating, abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, weight loss.
Signs of obstruction (distension, high-pitched bowel sounds in early CIPO, then hypoactive).

---

## Slide 14
Treatment:

CIPO: Nutritional support (TPN if needed), octreotide, erythromycin, neostigmine (hospital setting), surgical decompression if severe.
SMA syndrome: Weight restoration, postprandial positioning (knee-chest), duodenojejunostomy if refractory.

## Slide 15
4. Colonic Motility Disorders

Types:

Chronic constipation (slow transit, dyssynergic defecation)
Irritable bowel syndrome with constipation (IBS-C)
Colonic pseudo-obstruction (Ogilvie syndrome)

---

## Slide 16
Pathogenesis:

Slow transit: Reduced high-amplitude propagating contractions; often idiopathic, postinfectious, or neurodegenerative.
Dyssynergic defecation: Failure of pelvic floor relaxation + paradoxical anal contraction during attempted defecation.
Ogilvie syndrome: Acute colonic dilation without mechanical obstruction; associated with surgery, electrolytes, medications.

## Slide 18
Clinical Presentation:

Infrequent stools, straining, incomplete evacuation, manual maneuvers (dyssynergia).
Ogilvie: progressive abdominal distension, risk of perforation.

---

## Slide 19
Treatment:

Slow transit: Fiber, osmotic laxatives (PEG, lactulose), prucalopride (5-HT4 agonist), lubiprostone, linaclotide, colectomy (severe).
Dyssynergic defecation: Biofeedback therapy (first-line), pelvic floor physiotherapy.
Ogilvie: Neostigmine IV, colonoscopic decompression, surgical resection if perforated.

## Slide 20
5. Anorectal Motility Disorders

Type:
Hirschsprung disease (congenital)

---

## Slide 21
Pathogenesis (Hirschsprung):

Failure of neural crest cell migration → aganglionosis of distal rectum/colon → absent rectoanal inhibitory reflex (RAIR).

---

## Slide 23
Clinical Presentation:

Neonate: Failure to pass meconium within 48h, bilious vomiting, abdominal distension.
Adult (rare): Lifelong severe constipation, enterocolitis risk.
