1.  Introduction 
Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) refers to mucosal erosions occurring in the stomach or the first part of the 
duodenum. The condition arises from an imbalance between aggressive factors  (hydrochloric acid and 
pepsin injury)  and the protective mechanisms of the gastric and duodenal mucosa.

5.  H. pylori Infection 
Helicobacter pylori is a Gram-negative, microaerophilic spiral bacterium that colonises the gastric 
antrum in approximately 50% of the world's population. 
 
H. pylori — Key Facts 
• Gram-negative spiral bacterium colonising gastric mucosa (primarily antrum). 
• Produces urease: neutralizing local acid for survival. 
• Triggers chronic  gastritis, ulceration. 
• Virulence factors: cag A and vac A — associated with higher ulcer and gastric cancer risk. 
• Transmitted via oral-oral or faecal-oral routes; higher prevalence in developing countries. 
6.  NSAID-Induced Ulcers 
NSAIDs are the second most common cause of PUD. Their mechanisms of injury are both systemic 
and topical. 
 
Mechanism 
Consequence 
COX-1 inhibition 
Reduced prostaglandins E2 and I2 
Decreased prostaglandins 
Reduced mucus and bicarbonate secretion 
Topical epithelial effect 
Direct damage — NSAIDs accumulate in epithelial cells 
Reduced mucosal blood flow 
Impaired healing and increased susceptibility 
 
Risk Reduction Strategies 
• Use selective COX-2 inhibitors (e.g., celecoxib) where possible. 
• Co-prescribe a PPI (e.g., omeprazole 20 mg daily) with long-term NSAIDs. 
• Eradicate H. pylori before initiating chronic NSAID therapy. 
• Use the lowest effective NSAID dose for the shortest possible duration.

• Unintentional weight loss (>5% body weight). 
• Progressive dysphagia or odynophagia.

• Palpable epigastric mass or lymphadenopathy.

10.  Medical Management 
Key Principles 
• Eradicate H. pylori — cornerstone of treatment for H. pylori-positive ulcers. 
• Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) — most effective acid suppressants available. 
• Withdraw NSAIDs where clinically feasible. 
• Lifestyle modifications — stop smoking, limit alcohol, avoid late-night meals. 
• Confirm eradication: UBT or stool antigen >= 4 weeks after completing therapy.

Follow-Up

Excellent with successful eradication

13.  Take-Home Messages 
1.  PUD is common but highly treatable cause of upper GI pain. 
 
2.  H. pylori & NSAIDs are the dominant causes 
 
3.  PPIs + eradication = cornerstone of therapy 
 
4.  Complications demand urgent action 
 
5.  Prevention is possible by 
Avoiding NSAID, using gastroprotective agents, and            
H. pylori eradication, screening in high-risk groups.
