1. Esophageal Motility Disorders 
❖ Achalasia: Loss of inhibitory nitrergic neurons in the myenteric plexus → failure of lower esophageal 
sphincter (LES) relaxation + absent peristalsis. MCQ 
➢Treatment: Pneumatic dilation, peroral endoscopic myotomy (POEM), laparoscopic Heller myotomy, 
botulinum toxin (temporary). 
❖ Hypercontractile (Jackhammer esophagus): Imbalanced neural inhibition/excitation → simultaneous or 
excessively forceful contractions. 
➢Treatment: Calcium channel blockers, nitrates, botox, POEM for refractory cases. 
❖ Ineffective esophageal motility: Weak or failed peristalsis, often post-inflammatory or idiopathic. 
➢Treatment: Treat underlying GERD; prokinetics (limited efficacy). 
Clinical Presentation: 
 Dysphagia (both solids and liquids in achalasia; intermittent in DES) 
 Regurgitation, chest pain (DES, jackhammer) 
 Heartburn (IEM). 
 
2. Gastric Motility Disorders: Gastroparesis 
Pathogenesis: Impaired gastric emptying without mechanical obstruction. 
Causes: diabetic autonomic neuropathy, post-viral, post-surgical (vagal injury), idiopathic, Parkinson’s, 
 Cellular defects: loss of interstitial cells of Cajal (pacemakers), neuronal nitric oxide deficiency. 
Clinical Presentation: 
➢ Early satiety, postprandial fullness, nausea, vomiting, bloating, abdominal pain. 
➢ May lead to bezoars, malnutrition, glycemic instability (in diabetes). 
Treatment:  
 Dietary: Small, low-fat, low-fiber meals. 
 Pharmacologic: Metoclopramide (prokinetic + antiemetic), domperidone, erythromycin (short-term). 
 Refractory: Gastric peroral endoscopic myotomy (G-POEM), gastric electrical stimulation, pyloroplasty. 
 Supportive: Antiemetics (ondansetron, promethazine).

3.Small bowel motility disorder: 
CIPO (chronic intestinal pseudo-obstruction): Neuromuscular or myopathic failure (scleroderma, amyloidosis, 
paraneoplastic, mitochondrial disorders) → ineffective propulsion despite no mechanical blockage. 
➢Treatment: Nutritional support (TPN if needed), octreotide, erythromycin, neostigmine (hospital setting), 
surgical decompression if severe. 
Superior mesenteric artery syndrome: Loss of mesenteric fat pad (e.g., after rapid weight loss) → duodenal 
compression between aorta and SMA. 
➢Treatment: SMA syndrome: Weight restoration, postprandial positioning (knee-chest), duodenojejunostomy 
if refractory. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Clinical Presentation: 
 Chronic bloating, abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, weight loss. 
 Signs of obstruction (distension, high-pitched bowel sounds in early CIPO, then hypoactive). 
4.Colonic motility disorders: 
1.Chronic constipation (slow transit, dys-synergic defecation):  
Reduced high-amplitude propagating contractions; often idiopathic, postinfectious, or neurodegenerative. 
➢Treatment: Fiber, osmotic laxatives (PEG, lactulose), prucalopride (5-HT4 agonist), lubiprostone, linaclotide, 
colectomy (severe). 
 2.Dyssynergic defecation: 
  Failure of pelvic floor relaxation + paradoxical anal contraction during attempted defecation. 
Clinical Presentation: Infrequent stools, straining, incomplete evacuation, manual maneuvers (dyssynergia). 
➢Treatment: Biofeedback therapy (first-line), pelvic floor physiotherapy.

3.Ogilvie syndrome: 
 Acute colonic dilation without mechanical obstruction; associated with surgery, electrolytes, medications. 
clinical presentation: progressive abdominal distension, risk of perforation. 
➢Treatment: Neostigmine IV, colonoscopic decompression, surgical resection if perforated.

• 
Failure of neural crest cell migration → aganglionosis of distal rectum/colon → absent rectoanal 
inhibitory reflex (RAIR).   MCQ 
• 
Clinical Presentation: 
➢ Neonate: Failure to pass meconium within 48h, bilious vomiting, abdominal distension. 
➢ Adult (rare): Lifelong severe constipation, enterocolitis risk.
