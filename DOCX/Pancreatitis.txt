Pancreatitis
Pancreatitis is a pancreatic inflammatory disease. It may be classified into acute and chronic pancreatitis.
I. Acute Pancreatitis
An acute inflammatory process of the pancreas that can range from mild interstitial pancreatitis to severe necrotizing pancreatitis.
A. Etiology (Causes)
Gallstones (45%): Also known as biliary pancreatitis or gallstone pancreatitis.
Alcohol abuse (20%).
Idiopathic (10-30%).
Other causes include:
Medication: azathioprine, sulfonamides, tetracycline, valproic acid, thiazide, estrogens.
Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP): Known as Post-ERCP pancreatitis.
Hypercalcemia.
Hypertriglyceridemia.
Infections.
Genetic mutations.
Autoimmune pancreatitis.
Trauma: blunt or postoperative.
B. Diagnosis
1. Diagnostic Criteria
The diagnosis of acute pancreatitis is based on the fulfillment of two of the following three criteria:
Upper abdominal pain.
Elevated serum amylase or lipase (or both) of at least three times the upper limit of normal.
Findings consistent with acute pancreatitis on imaging (contrast-enhanced CT [CECT], MRI, or abdominal ultrasound).
2. Symptoms
Abdominal pain:
The major symptom, which is constant and steady.
Located in the epigastric, left upper quadrant, or periumbilical region.
Referred to the back.
Increased in the supine position and decreased by sitting and leaning forwards.
Varies from mild to severe.
Associated with nausea, vomiting, and abdominal distention.
3. Physical Examination
General Examination:
Low-grade fever, tachycardia, hypotension, or even shock (due to exudation of blood and plasma protein into the retroperitoneal space with hypovolemia).
Left pleural effusion (Reduced breath sounds and stony dull chest percussion suggest a pleural effusion).
Jaundice.
Local Abdominal Examination:
Abdominal tenderness with distention.
May have voluntary guarding or rigidity.
Abdominal ecchymoses around the umbilicus (Cullen's sign) and in both flanks (Turner’s sign) indicate severe necrotizing pancreatitis with hemoperitoneum.
Diminished or absent bowel sounds if ileus is present (due to chemical peritonitis).
4. Laboratory Investigations
Elevated serum amylase and/or serum lipase (serum amylase returns to normal after 2-3 days even with continuous inflammation, but serum lipase remains elevated for 7-14 days).
Leukocytosis, hyperglycemia, hypocalcemia, hypertriglyceridemia.
Increased serum bilirubin and Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP).
High Alanine Transaminase (ALT) and Aspartate Transaminase (AST) may indicate biliary etiology.
High Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH), low serum albumin, and high hematocrit > 44% (hemoconcentration) indicate more severe disease with a poor prognosis.
5. Imaging Investigations
Abdominal ultrasound: To evaluate the gallbladder for stones.
Contrast-enhanced CT (CECT): For detailed evaluation of the pancreas and surrounding structures (necrosis, collections, pseudocyst, tumors, vascular thromboses).
Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography (MRCP): For noninvasive evaluation of the pancreatico-biliary ductal system (small stones).
Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS): For better visualization of the pancreatico-biliary ductal system and pancreatic parenchyma.
C. Classification According to Severity
Mild: No local or systemic complications.
Moderate: Local complications or systemic complications without persistent organ failure.
Severe: Persistent single or multiple organ failure, which is associated with mortality rates between 20% and 40%.
D. Complications
1. Local Complications
The most frequent local complications are pancreatic or peripancreatic fluid collections.
Pancreatic pseudocyst.
Gastric outlet obstruction.
Splenic or portal vein thrombosis.
Details on Pancreatic Collections:
In Interstitial Pancreatitis: Collections contain a homogeneous liquid content and resolve spontaneously over time. If these collections persist beyond 4 weeks, they are referred to as pancreatic pseudocysts.
In Necrotizing Pancreatitis: Collections contain variable amounts of fluid and necrotic debris. Necrosis can involve the pancreatic parenchyma alone but is often accompanied by peripancreatic necrosis. When acute necrotic collections mature and encapsulate (usually after 4 weeks), they are referred to as walled-off necrosis.
2. Systemic Complications
Pulmonary: ARDS, respiratory failure.
Cardiovascular: Shock.
Renal: Renal failure.
Hematological: Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC).
Gastrointestinal: Bleeding.
Central Nervous System (CNS): Psychosis, fat embolism, and encephalopathy.
E. Differential Diagnosis
The differential diagnosis of acute severe upper abdominal pain includes:
Perforated peptic ulcer
Acute cholecystitis
Acute intestinal obstruction
Mesenteric vascular occlusion
Acute myocardial infarction
Pneumonia
Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)
F. Treatment
1. General Management
Symptom control.
Fluid replacement.
Early enteral feeding.
Close observation.
Antibiotics as needed.
Intervention in certain circumstances.
2. Specific Therapies
Fluid Resuscitation:
Rate: Intravenous fluid therapy with 5–10 mL/kg per hour.
Type: Ringer's lactate solution should be the preferred choice.
Pain Treatment and Nutrition:
Analgesics: NSAIDs, Opioids, or epidural anesthesia, according to the degree of pain.
Nutrition: Enteral tube feeding and early oral feeding should be initiated after 48 hours.
Role of ERCP:
ERCP is only indicated in patients with biliary pancreatitis and concomitant cholangitis.
Role of Antibiotics:
Used only in confirmed or clinically suspected secondary infection.
3. Management of Local Complications
Conservative Treatment: The majority of patients with sterile (with no secondary infection) pancreatic or peripancreatic necrosis, fluid collections, and pancreatic pseudocysts can be treated conservatively because most will resolve spontaneously.
Interventional Treatment:
Infected Necrotizing Pancreatitis:
Secondary infection of peripancreatic necrosis nearly always requires invasive intervention.
Step-up treatment:
Broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy.
Percutaneous catheter drainage.
Endoscopic drainage.
Minimally invasive necrosectomy.
Pancreatic Pseudocyst:
Intervention is indicated if the pseudocyst causes gastric outlet obstruction, refractory pain, or is larger than 6 cm in diameter.
Treatment is by either endoscopic or surgical drainage.
G. Prevention of Recurrence
Approximately 20% of patients with acute biliary pancreatitis develop recurrent attacks. After mild biliary pancreatitis, cholecystectomy during the same hospital admission is strongly advised.
Other important risk factors for recurrence and progression to chronic pancreatitis are alcohol consumption and smoking. Alcohol abstinence after the first episode protects against disease recurrence.
II. Chronic Pancreatitis
A disease process characterized by irreversible damage to the pancreas with histological abnormalities including chronic inflammation, fibrosis, and progressive destruction of both exocrine and eventually endocrine tissue.
A. Causes
Alcohol: The most common cause in adults.
Idiopathic: (Unknown cause) in 25% of cases.
Other causes include:
Cystic fibrosis: The commonest cause in children; an autosomal recessive disorder with mutations in the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulation (CFTR) gene.
Autoimmune pancreatitis.
Hereditary: In 15% of cases, due to a genetic defect in the gene encoding for trypsinogen.
Congenital anomalies in the pancreas.
B. Diagnosis
1. Symptoms and Signs
Abdominal pain: Variable in severity from mild to severe, may be constant or intermittent. Eating may exacerbate the pain.
Maldigestion: Manifested as chronic diarrhea, steatorrhea, weight loss, and fatigue.
Unremarkable abdominal examination in most cases, or there may be mild abdominal tenderness.
Diabetes Mellitus (DM): Polyuria, polydipsia, fatigue, or complications of DM.
2. Laboratory Investigations
Serum amylase and lipase are usually not elevated.
Increased bilirubin and ALP may be due to cholestasis (from a stricture in the common bile duct).
Decreased serum trypsinogen level.
Decreased fecal elastase.
Genetic tests.
3. Imaging Investigations
Plain X-ray of the abdomen: Can detect diffuse pancreatic calcification.
Abdominal US: Can show pancreatic calcifications.
Contrast-enhanced abdominal CT: Can show a dilated pancreatic duct, pancreatic calcifications, and atrophy.
MRI / MRCP: Can show ductal and parenchymal changes.
Endoscopic US (EUS): Can show ductal dilation, calcification, cysts, and masses.
C. Complications
Diabetes Mellitus (DM).
Cholestasis and biliary cirrhosis.
Pancreatic adenocarcinoma (4% risk), which is more common in the hereditary type.
D. Treatment
For Maldigestion and Steatorrhea:
Pancreatic enzymes are the cornerstone of treatment.
Dosage is 40,000–50,000 units of pancreatic lipase per meal, and the dose should be increased until symptoms are relieved.
For Pain:
Serine Proteases: These lower Cholecystokinin (CCK) levels to decrease pancreatic secretions.
Octreotide: A somatostatin analogue that inhibits pancreatic secretion (subcutaneous injection of 200 ug three times daily).
Ductal decompression by endoscopic stenting of a dominant stricture.
Total pancreatectomy if abdominal pain is refractory to conventional treatment.
